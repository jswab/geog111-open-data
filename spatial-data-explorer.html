Advantages of open spatial data
🌐No cost barrier›
Anyone can download and use open datasets regardless of budget — a student, an NGO in a low-income country, or a solo researcher. This fundamentally levels the playing field for who gets to conduct spatial analysis.
Example: A university student can download the full OpenStreetMap planet file (~80 GB) for free and run nationwide analysis without any subscription.
🔁Freely shareable & reusable›
Open licences such as the Open Database Licence (ODbL) allow you to publish maps, share derived datasets with partners, and build commercial products — as long as you attribute the source and keep derivatives open.
Example: The Humanitarian OpenStreetMap Team (HOT) freely shares crisis-mapping data with UN agencies and first responders worldwide without legal restrictions.
👥Community-verified accuracy›
Millions of contributors edit OpenStreetMap daily. Errors are spotted and corrected by local mappers who know their area best. In many dense urban regions, OSM is more detailed and more current than commercial alternatives.
Example: In Jakarta, OSM building footprints are more complete than many commercial datasets, maintained by a large and active local mapping community.
📚Enables reproducible research›
Science depends on reproducibility. If your spatial analysis uses openly licensed data, any peer can re-run your workflow exactly. Proprietary data cannot be bundled with published research, making independent verification impossible.
Example: Papers using Copernicus land-cover data or GADM boundaries can be fully reproduced by any reader — critical for peer review and policy influence.
🕓Deep historical archives›
The USGS Landsat archive stretches back to 1972 — over 50 years of free, consistent satellite imagery. This historical depth is unmatched by any commercial provider and is essential for studying long-term environmental change.
Example: Global Forest Watch uses the free Landsat archive to track deforestation since the 1980s — a project that would be financially impossible using paid imagery alone.
🏛️Government-backed reliability›
Many open datasets are produced by national mapping agencies or space agencies (OS, USGS, ESA, NASA). They are not going to vanish because a startup loses funding, and their collection methodologies are publicly documented.
Example: Ordnance Survey OpenData boundary files have been maintained for decades and underpin UK government statistics and planning without interruption.
Limitations of open spatial data
📍Uneven global coverage›
Crowd-sourced data reflects where contributors live. OSM is extraordinarily detailed in Western Europe and North America but can be sparse in rural Central Africa, Central Asia, or conflict zones — exactly where geographic data is often most needed.
Impact: An NGO mapping healthcare access in South Sudan may find OSM road data too incomplete for routing analysis, requiring costly commercial alternatives or expensive field surveys.
🧹Requires significant preprocessing›
Raw government shapefiles often use inconsistent projections, outdated attribute schemas, mixed geometry types, and encoding errors. Significant cleaning and transformation is routinely required before any analysis can begin.
Impact: Joining administrative boundary files from multiple countries for a continental analysis can require days of work aligning schemas, fixing topology errors, and reconciling CRS differences.
🛰️Lower imagery resolution›
The best freely available satellite imagery — Sentinel-2 at 10m, Landsat at 30m — is too coarse for many urban applications. Detecting individual buildings, vehicles, or infrastructure damage typically requires sub-metre resolution, available only commercially.
Impact: Counting solar panels on rooftops, assessing post-earthquake building damage, or estimating crop yield at field level all require commercial imagery at 30cm–3m resolution.
⏱️Update lag›
Many government datasets are updated annually, or even less frequently. For rapidly changing environments — post-disaster zones, fast-growing informal settlements, or busy logistics networks — free data may be months out of date.
Impact: A humanitarian organisation responding to a flood needs current road passability data. An OSM extract from last month may not reflect roads newly damaged or cleared.
🤷No quality guarantee or support›
Open datasets come with no SLA. There is no customer support team to call, no guaranteed uptime for download servers, and no legal recourse if data quality leads to a flawed decision. You carry the risk.
Impact: An engineering firm using open elevation data for infrastructure design carries the quality risk itself. A paid provider's accuracy guarantee provides legal protection that free data cannot.
⚖️Licence complexity›
"Free" doesn't mean "no conditions". ODbL requires share-alike on derived databases. Some government open-data licences restrict commercial use. Mixing datasets under different licences can create legal conflicts that are hard to unpick.
Impact: A company building a commercial routing app with OSM must ensure all derived data stays open — they cannot close-source a proprietary speed dataset they've merged with OSM roads.

Advantages of proprietary spatial data
🔬Very high resolution imagery›
Maxar's WorldView-3 delivers 30cm resolution — you can identify individual cars, solar panels, damage to specific buildings, or crop rows. This level of detail simply does not exist in any free satellite source.
Example: Insurance companies use Maxar imagery to assess individual property damage after storms without dispatching physical assessors — a major cost and safety advantage.
⚡Near real-time capture›
Planet's constellation of ~200 satellites images the entire Earth daily. Sentinel-2 revisits every 5 days and can be clouded. For monitoring active wildfires, flood extents, or supply-chain infrastructure, daily imagery is often essential.
Example: Agricultural companies use Planet daily imagery to detect crop stress within 24 hours, enabling targeted irrigation before yields are lost.
✅Contractual quality guarantee›
Paid providers commit to positional accuracy, attribute completeness, and geometry validity via SLAs. If data errors cause financial loss, there is legal recourse. This is critical for infrastructure projects, insurance underwriting, and emergency services.
Example: A utility company using HERE road data for network planning has contractual accuracy assurances — essential when errors could send maintenance crews to the wrong location.
🗺️Globally uniform coverage›
Commercial providers collect data everywhere deliberately, with consistent quality across all regions. HERE's road network covers 200+ countries at the same schema and attribute richness — something crowd-sourced data cannot guarantee.
Example: A multinational logistics firm needs consistent turn-restriction and speed-limit data across 60 countries. Only commercial providers deliver this at uniform quality.
🛎️Professional support & SLAs›
Paying for data buys access to technical support teams, onboarding specialists, and guaranteed API uptime. When a critical production system fails at 3am, you can call someone with contractual obligations to help.
Example: Ride-hailing apps using HERE or TomTom maps have dedicated account managers and SLAs ensuring map updates are delivered within agreed timeframes.
🏆Competitive advantage›
If your organisation can access a dataset competitors cannot afford, it confers genuine informational advantage. Hedge funds pay millions for satellite-derived footfall data at retail sites that nobody else can access.
Example: Orbital Insight sells satellite-derived car park occupancy counts at retail stores to investment funds, who use it to predict quarterly earnings before official announcements.
Limitations of proprietary spatial data
💰Prohibitively expensive›
Maxar imagery tasking runs $500–$3,000 per scene. Planet full-archive access costs tens of thousands per year. These costs exclude most researchers, NGOs, and small organisations — the people who often need quality spatial data most.
Alternative: For large-area analysis, Sentinel-2 (free, 10m) or Landsat (free, 30m) may be entirely sufficient — at zero cost.
🔒Strict licensing restrictions›
You typically cannot publish, redistribute, or share the raw data, include it in academic papers, or use it outside the specific purposes in your contract. Accidental re-sharing can trigger legal action and significant financial penalties.
Alternative: Open data under ODbL or CC-BY can be freely shared, published, and built upon — no legal risk in collaboration or publication.
📦Vendor lock-in›
Building workflows around a proprietary API or data schema creates deep dependency. If a provider discontinues a product, raises prices, or is acquired, migrating to an alternative is costly in time, money, and lost institutional knowledge.
Alternative: Open formats (GeoJSON, GeoPackage, Cloud-Optimised GeoTIFF) and open data enable portability — your workflows aren't tied to any single vendor's API.
🌍Deepens knowledge inequality›
The regions where quality spatial data is most critical for development decisions are also where governments and organisations are least able to pay for it. This creates a self-reinforcing cycle of spatial data poverty.
Alternative: Initiatives like HOT OSM and the World Bank's Open Data programme deliberately try to close this gap using freely available and collaboratively produced data.
🔭Opaque collection methods›
Commercial providers rarely disclose full details of how data was collected, processed, or validated — it's proprietary. This makes independent quality assessment difficult and can create unexpected errors in downstream analysis.
Alternative: Government open datasets (USGS, ESA) come with full technical documentation, sensor specifications, and processing chains that can be independently audited.
🚫Blocks reproducible science›
Academic and policy research built on proprietary data cannot be independently reproduced — other researchers cannot access the same inputs. Journals increasingly require data to be publicly archived, which is impossible with licensed commercial data.
Alternative: Research using Sentinel, Landsat, or OSM data can be fully reproduced by any reader, strengthening peer review and public trust in findings.
